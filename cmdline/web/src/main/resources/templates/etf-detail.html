Alpha measures returns relative to a benchmark index. It is a number (3 or -5), that refers to a percentage (i.e. 3% better or 5% worse).

Beta indicates whether the investment is more or less volatile than the market as a whole. A beta of 1 indicates the price moves with the market. A beta of less than 1 means that the price is less volatile than the market. A beta of greater than 1 means the price is more volatile than the market. .

R-squared measures a fund performance versus its benchmark index. Values range from 0 to 100%. Values from 70 to 100 indicate good correlation. 40 to 70 indicate average correlation. Below 40 indicates poor correlation.

Beta should only be used when a security has a high R-squared value. For example, if a fund has an R-squared value of close to 100% but has a beta below 1, it is most likely offering higher risk-adjusted returns.

Deviation depicts historical volatility. A volatile portfolio will have a higher standard deviation than a less volatile portfolio.

Sharpe Ratio measures the excess return compared to treasury bills. The higher the number the better the performance.

Treynor Ratio measures the excess return compared to the whole market. The higher the number the better the performance.

PE measures the current share price relative to the per-share earnings.

PS compares a company’s stock price to its revenues. A low ratio may indicate possible undervaluation, while a ratio that is significantly above the average may suggest overvaluation.

PC measures an equities market value to its cash flow. Look for smaller numbers.

PB measures an equities market value to its book value.
